# Where I Started (Grade 9)

My love for programming began long before I started high school when I first got into web development during the COVID pandemic in 2020. What started as a fun hobby would eventually turn into a passion that resulted in me receiving multiple opportunities that led me to getting into Software Engineering.

Majority of my middle school life consisted of dealing with the COVID pandemic and the subsequent multiple lockdowns. As a result, there were not many initiatives at my school to participate in compared to later in my middle school years. Most of my extracurriculars at that time were composed of various programming projects.

In Grade 9, I wasn't really thinking about university. I was a kid who simply went to school, studied and had fun there, and went home to play video games or tinker with a project.

Sometime in the middle of the year, I began to hear conversations about what people wanted to do after high school and what university they wanted to pursue their education at. These concepts were pretty abstract to me at the time, but I began to research some universities in Canada that I could attend for Computer Science and/or Software Engineering.

In my search, a couple of names were quite popular: Dalhousie University, McGill University, University of Waterloo, University of Toronto, and The University of British Colombia.

But one stood out for a very specific reason. Out of all universities, the University of Waterloo was exceptional for its world-class co-op program. I am someone who loves to be hands-on and industry oriented compared to research, so this was very appealing to me.

But the problem was that getting into the CS/SWE programs at Waterloo wasn't the most easy task. Not only did you need exceptional grades in high school (97%+), you needed programming experience and extracurriculars as well. At the time, I had decent grades, some programming experience, but no extracurriculars to my name.

I began to think of ways I can optimize my application in a reasonable manner (considering it was Grade 9) to make myself an appealing candidate for the university. One way I could immediately get involved was through the University of Waterloo's mathematics contests. I wrote one contest that year, which was the Pascal Contest.

I prepared weeks in advance for this contest and wrote it at my school. I scored a 97/150 on the contest, which was an above average score but nothing exceptional.

I was quite bored with my time at school and was searching for some fulfilling extracurriculars in my final year of middle school. One that stood out to me, but one that I was reluctant to do was improv. Improv is the art of acting without a script, which is a very fun but challenging group initiative.

I was someone who was very averse to public speaking. When granted any opportunities, I would avoid it at all costs. I can say that joining improv was one of the best decisions I made for myself, as it was a significant reason why I gained a lot of confidence in speaking in front large crowds of people.

I made the competitive team for my middle school, and we ended up winning 1st in the Halifax regional competition, one of my first ever competition wins.

At the end of my year, I graduated middle school and was excited about the prospect of moving into high school. I realized that I had to really make the most out of the next three years to achieve my goals.

# Finding my Niche (Grade 10)

Before starting high school, I needed some sort of mental note that explains how I could work towards getting into Waterloo. I created the following:

- Math contests (Euclids etc..)
- Coding contests
- Side projects - Github, FOSS
- Regular coding
- High grades (>90%)
- ECs (extracurriculars)
- Math, English, Science + comp sci

Over the next three years, I wanted to write UW's math contests, create coding projects that showed of my skills, achieve high grades, and participate in quality extracurriculars.

At the time, I wasn't quite sure how I was going to juggle all of these different components to the point I was a little lost in the initial few months of grade 10.

On my first day of high school, I noticed that our student council was looking for helping hands for events and initiatives they ran. I joined immediately, writing a letter of intent expressing my interests and my reasons for why I was an ideal candidate.

I got into Student Council, but my advisor noticed something interesting about my letter of intent. Within it, I accentuated my passion for technology, which caught her eye because there was a need for a Technology Club within my school.

I realized that this could be the extracurricular that could very well be the defining part of my application, as it allows me to take my passion of technology and blend it with community impact.

I wasn't sure of how to run a club, so I planned what sort of things the club would do in a very simple mission statement:

> “Exploring tech together, making it fun and accessible for everyone in our school”

The club would conduct meetings every week on a Wednesday, where I would explore a technical concept with the club and we will do some hands-on work and have fun with it!

For the year, I mostly focused on web development, my first love, as it offered a way for people to jump in programming while seeing the results of their work visually. In less than a month I was able to get a consistent attendance of 30 people in a classroom, rarely ever seen in a club meeting before.

I was very ecstatic to see a large amount of people who shared the same passion for technology that I did, and continued on with teaching web development for the rest of the year.
